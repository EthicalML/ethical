1. Human Augmentation

I commit to assess the impact of incorrect predictions and, when reasonable, design systems with human-in-the-loop review processes

When introducing automation through machine learning systems, it's easy to forget the impact that wrong predictions can have in full end-to-end automation.

Technologists should understand the consequences of incorrect predictions, especially when automating critical processes that can have significant impact in human lives (e.g. justice, health, transport, etc)

However this isn't limited to obvious critical use-cases - enabling subject-domain-experts as human-in-the-loop reviewers at the end of ML systems can have significant benefits.

1. Human Aumentation

What are some examples where I should look towards adding human-in-the-loop review processes?

Automatic Prision Sentences

Deploying machine learning systems that sentence humans to prision sentences is an obvious example of a system that should be deployed carefuly. Especially if the inner workings of the model cannot be assessed.

These is a prime example where a human-in-the-loop process design is necessary, augmenting the capabilities of subject-domain-experts providing them with the relevant information and automating the work of collecting and processing data which would otherwise be manual

Government Officials

Be at the forefront of industry disruption with AI, obtain first-hand experience on practical implementations of machine learning in industry, and have the opportunity to define the best practices for AI development across multiple sectors.

Executives

Obtain access to an elite network of action oriented AI leaders and be at the forefront leading the way on building frameworks in the workplace that ensure the ethical development of ML, and take the lead on shaping the future of practical policy in AI, Data and Machine Learning.

2. Bias Evaluation

I commit to continuously develop processes that allow me to understand and evaluate bias in development and production.

When building systems that have to make non-trivial decisions, we will always face an inherent bias - it is impossible to avoid.

However, technologists should understand the implications of the bias present, and based on that make sure we can take reasonable precautions and approaches to minimise undesired bias when training a model, including data labelling, simulation or generation efforts

Similarly, once the model is deployed in production, it is important that we establish a way to monitor these models for potential biases that may arise and act accordingly.

3. Displacement Strategy

I commit to always consider and propose ideas to mitigate the impact relevant workers will face when medium to large-scale processes are being automated

When rolling out automation systems, technologists should take into consideration the implications for workers that it will be automating, and develop a plan accordingly.

Developing rollout / deployment plans should take the organisation- and industry-level impact into consideration, and should be addressed irrespective of the background of workers (i.e. skilled or otherwise).

When this is addressed from the design stages, it is often possible to even have a positive impact on the rollout of the technology.

4. Practical Accuracy

I commit to build understanding around accuracy and cost metrics from a practical domain-specific perspective

When building systems that learn from data, it is important to obtain a thorough understanding on the underlying means to assess accuracy.

Often it is not enough just using plain accuracy or default/basic cost metrics as what may be "correct" for a computer, may be "wrong" for a human (and viceversa).

Ensuring the right challenge is being addressed in the right way can be achieved by breaking down the implications of f-1 score metrics from a domain-specific perspective, as well as exploring alternative cost functions based on domain-knowledge.

5. Compliance by design

I commit to improve transparency and explainability of machine learning models where reasonable

With the deep learning and machine learning hype, it may be tempting to always throw large amounts of data into complex machine learning pipelines, however technologists should ensure there is a fundamental understanding and ability to explain the results based on features and models chosen.

It is possible to use different tools and approaches to make models more explainable, such as by adding domain knowledge through features themselves - although on certain situations accuracy may decrease, the transparency and explainability gains may be significant.

6. Reproducible systems

I commit to continuously improve my machine learning infrastructure to enable for a reasonable level of reproducibility

Often production machine learning systems don' t have the capabilities to diagnose effectively when something bad happens with a model - let alone, reproduce the same results.

In production systems, it is important to perform standard procedures, such as reverting a model to a previous version, or reproducing an input to debug a specific functionality

Fortunately there is a lot of great work around reproducibility in data science, including various ways to build audit trails, store computational graphs, store data as it is transformed through a pipeline, etc. which should be adopted to have reasonable level of reproducibility.

7. Trust beyond user

I commit to build and communicate processes that protect and handle data with stakeholders that may interact with the system directly and/or indirectly

When developing large-scale systems that learn from data, there are often large number of stakeholders that may be affected directly and indirectly.

Building trust within relevant stakeholders is not only done through informing what data is being held, but also with the processes around the data, as well as the understanding of why protecting the data is important

Technologists should enforce privacy by design across systems, as well as continuous processes to build trust not only with users, but also relevant stakeholders such as procurement frameworks, operational users, and beyond.

8. Data risk awareness

I commit to develop and improve relevant processes and infrastructure to ensure data and security is taken into consideration during the development of machine learning systems

Autonomous decision-making systems open the doors to new potential security breaches.

More importantly, it is critical to be aware that most security breaches occur due to human error as opposed to actual hacks (i.e. someone sending the dataset attached in an email by accident, or losing their laptop/phone)

Nevertheless, it is important to make sure we prepare for both through explicit efforts to educate relevant personel, establish relevant processes around data, and assess implications of ML backdoors (such as adversarial attacks).
