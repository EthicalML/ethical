1. Human Augmentation

I commit to assess benefits from a human-in-the-loop designs, instead of fully automating processes without understanding impact

When introducing automation through machine learning systems, it's easy to forget the impact that wrong predictions can have in end-to-end automation.

It is important to assess the consequences of incorrect predictions, especially in systems that automate processes in justice, healthcare, transport, and such.

However this isn't limited to obvious critical use-cases - it is important for technologists to understand how human-in-the-loop design can exponentially increase their end-to-end functionality.

2. Bias Evaluation

I commit to build processes that allow me to understand and evaluate bias in development and production.

When building systems that have to make non-trivial decisions, we will always face an inherent bias - it is impossible to avoid.

However, it is important that we are able to identify potential bias implications, and build processes that allow us to evaluate during the development of our models

Similarly, once the model is deployed in production, it is important that we establish a way to monitor these model for potential biases that may arise and act accordingly.

3. Displacement Strategy

I commit to understand disruption implications when rolling out automation systems and propose plans to mitigate impact

When rolling out automation systems, it is important to take into consideration the implications that this may have, and develop a plan accordingly.

The deployment of automation systems may affect various stakeholders - it is important that the impact of this is assessed, as otherwise this may have unintended consequences.

When this is addressed from the design stages, it provides a positive impact on the rollout of the technology, so there is large incentive for this.

4. Practical Accuracy

I commit to obtain practical understanding on the effect accuracy and cost metrics would have from a practical perspective

When building systems that learn from data, it is important to obtain a thorough understanding on the underlying means to assess accuracy.

Often it is not enough just using plain accuracy or default/basic cost metrics as what may be "correct" for a computer, may be "wrong" for a human - or viceversa.

It is important to break down the impact of f-1 score metrics, as well as alternative cost functions based on human domain to ensure the right challenge is being addressed in the right way.

5. Compliance by design

I commit to develop approaches to improve transparency and explainability of machine learning models where reasonable

With the deep learning and machine learning hype, it may be tempting to always throw large amounts of data into complex machine learning pipelines, however it is important to ensure there is an understanding on the data, features and models being used.

It is possible to use different tools and approaches to make models more explainable, such as through the features, or by adding domain knowledge through features themselves - although on certain situations accuracy may decrease, the transparency and explainability gains may be significant.

6. Reproducibile systems

I commit to continuously improve my machine learning infrastructure to allow for a reasonable level of reproducibility

Often, data-based production systems don't have the capabilities to assess when something bad happens with a model - let alone, reproduce the same results.

In production systems, it is necessary to be ready to perform standard processes, such as reverting a model to a previous version, or reproducing an input to debug a specific functionality

Fortunately, there are a growing number of best practice approaches to reproducibility in machine learning, including various ways to build audit trails, save computational states, store data as it is transformed through an ETL pipeline, etc.

7. Trust beyond user

I commit to identify and build processes to identify and communicate with stakeholders that may interact with the system directly or indirectly

When developing large-scale systems that learn from data, there are often large number of stakeholders that may be affected.

Building trust within relevant stakeholders is not only done through informing what data is being held, but also with the processes around the data, as well as the understanding of why protecting the data is important

It is important to enforce privacy by design across systems, as well as continuous processes to build trust not only with users, but also relevant stakeholders such as procurement frameworks, operational users, and beyond.

8. Data risk awareness

I commit to develop and improve relevant processes and infrastructure to ensure data and security is taken into consideration during data science / machine learning development

Autonomous decision-making systems open the doors to new potential security breaches.

More importantly, knowing that most security breaches occur due to human error - someone sending the dataset attached in an email by accident, or losing their laptop/phone - as opposed to large-scale hacks

Nevertheless, it is important to make sure we prepare for both through explicit efforts to educate relevant personel, establish relevant processes around data, and asses implications of ML backdoors (such as adversarial attacks).
